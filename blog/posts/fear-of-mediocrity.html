The Fear of Mediocrity

04/27/2026 Philosophy Relationships

Intro

I recently turned 22, and I'm now in my fourth year of college at UCSD. When I first entered college, I was a completely different person. I had this deep internal drive that I saw as part of my core identity. People would often ask me, "How are you so driven and motivated?" or tell me that I inspired them and that I was smart. As corny as it sounds, where other people saw risk, I often saw opportunity.

I was disciplined, thoughtful, unimpressionable, and calculated. I had a firm grasp on my beliefs, my actions, and the direction I wanted my life to take. It felt like I was the master of my own mind and body. If I had a goal, I was accomplishing it, regardless of how many barriers I faced, how difficult it was. I fully believed in myself.

Now, I want to examine why I no longer feel that same sense of control. I want to understand what changed, whether that change was good or bad, and what it means to lose or possibly outgrow a version of yourself that once felt so strong.

The Beginning

I read a quote online that said, "Effortless self-discipline happens when the desire to become the highest version of yourself outweighs the desires of the lowest version of yourself."

That quote describes the version of me who entered college. At that time, self-discipline felt effortless. I rejected anything that did not fit into my playbook for becoming the highest version of myself. My old self would never go out for drinks. I would never eat fast food. I cooked healthy meals every day, cut expenses so I could invest the extra money, and exercised daily no matter how late it was. I would not let myself sleep until I had gone to the gym. There were nights when I found myself lifting weights at 2 a.m.

People often called me crazy because of how hard I worked. I viewed it as if I had no other choice. This was my baseline. I self-studied eight to twelve hours a day just to catch up on everything I had fallen behind on throughout high school. At the time, I didn't know what to make of their reactions.

But this is how I see it now:

If people are calling you crazy because of how you live, that is one of the clearest signs that you are doing something right.

To the people calling me crazy, my lifestyle truly did seem crazy. It was three standard deviations away from their baseline. They could not understand why I worked so hard, what drove me, or why it came so naturally to me.

How It All Changed

Coming into college, I knew that making friends and forming relationships was part of the experience. I wanted to make friends too. I tried to surround myself with people who were like-minded, ambitious, and driven, and I did for the most part.

But college also introduced me to a different culture. Coming from Oregon to California, I met a lot of people who came from wealthier backgrounds, people who did not seem to take school as seriously, and people who had essentially lived their entire life on easy mode. Not to say that my life was been extremely difficult, but I certainly did not come from easy mode growing up. Now I was around other college students, people who seemed successful, intelligent, and socially accepted. Because of that, I felt that the opinions of others on my lifestyle were more valid.

And the general culture of being in your twenties, especially as a college student, does not exactly reward extreme discipline. It rewards looseness. It rewards spontaneity. It rewards going out, drinking, taking shortcuts, being casual, and not trying too hard. The person I was at 17 or 18 did not fit neatly into that culture. And people felt comfortable telling me that.

Over time, I started listening.

People told me I needed to "loosen up." They told me I should go out every once in a while. They told me not everything had to be so serious. They told me I did not need to put 100% effort into everything. At first, I resisted. But little by little, those words got in.

Some of my friends noticed before I did. They could tell I was slipping. They were frustrated with me because they remembered who I was. They knew what I was capable of. They knew I was becoming less disciplined, less focused, less committed to the version of myself I had worked so hard to build. But by then, their words could not reach me. I had already internalized the voices telling me to ease up, to care less, to expect less from myself, to become less.

From 19 to 22, I became normal.

I started fitting in. I went out more. I drank more. I studied less (still got straight A's though). I let my standards soften. The intrinsic drive I once had slowly faded until it felt almost foreign to me. School became a chore. Exercise became a chore. Working hard became a chore.

And the strangest part is that I let it happen because I thought that was what I was supposed to do.

Everyone around me seemed to be saying, directly or indirectly, that this was what life in your twenties was supposed to look like. You go out. You relax. You shouldn't be so stubborn. You stop treating every day like it matters. You become more "balanced".

And I thought to myself, "Balance sounds nice, maybe I should give it a try."

And that was when my baseline shifted.

My baseline had shifted to that of the people who once called me crazy.

And from that shifted perspective, I could finally understand them. I was no longer the crazy one because I was no longer living in a way that made me hard to understand. If I see someone working fourteen hours a day now, I think they are crazy too. But the painful irony is that this was exactly who I was three years ago. The very lifestyle that once felt natural to me now feels almost unimaginable. The person I once was would look extreme to the person I have become.

And that realization hurts.

Because the version of me that was once deeply afraid of mediocrity slowly became the definition of mediocrity.

Not because I made one dramatic mistake. Not because I suddenly gave up. But because I allowed my standards to be negotiated downward, one small compromise at a time.

I confused becoming social with becoming less serious. I confused loosening up with letting go. I confused fitting in with growing up.

And somewhere in that process, I lost the part of myself that used to believe effort was not a burden, but a way of life.

The version of me that was once so afraid of mediocrity was worn down slowly, through comfort, compromise, and the desire to be understood by people who never shared my urgency in the first place.

Now What?

Now I am 22 years old, in my fourth year of college, trying to make sense of what changed, and where to go from here.

I will say this: I have not lost all of my spark. I still perform well in school. I still think deeply. I still care about the direction of my life. But I am no longer operating from the same internal fire that once made discipline feel effortless. But there must be some lesson to learn from the changes I've gone through.

The way I see it now, the change has been a net negative in terms of achieving my goals and becoming the best version of myself. I am not going to pretend otherwise. I am less driven than I used to be. I am less disciplined. I am less willing to sacrifice comfort for progress.

But at the same time, I do think I gained something. I gained perspective. I now understand the people who once called me crazy. I understand the average person's baseline because, in some ways, I have lived inside of it. I understand how easy it is to choose comfort, how easy it is to normalize lower standards, and how difficult it is to maintain an intense vision of yourself when the world around you constantly asks you to relax.

That perspective is valuable. But I do not want to stay here. I want my drive back. I want to fear mediocrity again. I want to win. Not in some shallow, ego-driven way, but because I want to accomplish something meaningful with my life. I want to become someone I respect. And I believe that people who accomplish great things are, in some deep way, afraid of becoming average. They are afraid of wasting their potential. They are afraid of looking back and realizing they became less than they could have been.

How to Move Forward

The answer is, albeit obvious, that I have to shift my baseline back up, little by little.

The problem is that I have let my current baseline sit for too long. I have gotten comfortable here. And comfort is dangerous because, after a while, it stops feeling like comfort and starts feeling like identity.

This isn't just be about going to the gym more often or eating healthier meals. Those things matter, but they are surface-level compared to the deeper issue. If I want to return to a higher baseline, I have to rebuild the traits that made that version of me possible in the first place. I have to become disciplined, thoughtful, unimpressionable, and calculated again.

But I also know I cannot change everything all at once. That would be a recipe for failure. I cannot wake up tomorrow and simply become the 18-year-old version of myself again. That person was built through habits, standards, beliefs, and hardship. If I want to become that driven again, I have to rebuild myself gradually, strategically, and with intention.

I have to treat my life like a gameboard. I have to identify what is helping me, what is weakening me, what is raising my baseline, and what is lowering it. Then I have to make moves.

One of the harder truths is that some of my relationships tie me to the version of myself I am trying to outgrow. There are people in my life who, intentionally or not, pull my baseline downward. They normalize the habits I am trying to escape. They make it harder to become serious again.

That does not mean they are bad people. Many of them are good people. But good people can still be bad for your direction. Someone can be kind, funny, and meaningful to you while still reinforcing a version of yourself that you no longer want to be.

This is where things become difficult for me. I think I am a sympathetic person, and that sympathy can become a weakness. I can understand people's struggles so much that I let their standards become my own. I can care about people so much that I allow myself to be pulled down with them. I do not want to abandon people, but I also cannot keep sacrificing my future self just to preserve comfort in the present.

The cold, calculated version of me would have recognized this immediately. He would have identified the largest inhibitors and removed them one by one. He would not have over-explained. He would not have hesitated. He would have made the necessary move.

But I don't think that is correct. That's the easy way out. It's selfish and cruel. I want to be better than that. So I must figure out, how do I become that person again without taking shortcuts and acting selfish? That is what I have been struggling to navigate, and the reason I'm here writing this.

I do not think the answer is to return completely to who I was. I think the answer is to recover the fire, discipline, and fear of mediocrity that once drove me, while carrying the perspective I gained from losing them.

I have seen both baselines now. I know what it feels like to live far above average, and I know what it feels like to slowly become average. That knowledge should mean something.

Now the task is to choose, deliberately, which direction I want my life to move.

Conclusion

I hope this helped anyone who wants to become more like their best self. See you next time!
